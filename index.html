üí∞ Why Measure Client Profitability?

Not all revenue is created equal. A $10,000 client who takes 200 hours is less profitable than a $5,000 client who takes 30 hours.

When you measure profitability per client, you discover who's worth keeping, who needs a rate increase, and who you might need to fire.

üìä The 80/20 rule: Often 80% of your profit comes from 20% of your clients. The rest may be breaking even ‚Äî or worse, costing you money.

üíº Pro Tips From the Field

  • Track ALL time, not just billable. Meetings, emails, revisions ‚Äî it all counts. Clients often look profitable until you count the "free" work.
  • Set a target effective rate. If your goal is $100/hour, any client below that is costing you opportunity. You could be serving higher-paying clients instead.
  • Include direct costs. If you hire a contractor or buy software specifically for a client, that reduces their profitability.
  • Review quarterly. Client profitability changes over time. A great client can become unprofitable (or vice versa). Check regularly.
  • Fire the bottom 10-20%. Controversial but effective. Dropping your worst clients frees time to find better ones ‚Äî or improves your quality of life.
  • Raise rates on marginal clients. If they're barely profitable, a rate increase fixes it or they leave (solving the problem either way).
  • Factor in stress and enjoyment. A slightly less profitable client who's easy to work with may be worth keeping over a profitable nightmare.
